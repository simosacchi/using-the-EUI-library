Research data

We take an inclusive notion of research data at the core of our work:

Research data is all information intentionally collected, observed, generated or reused to validate research findings and substantiate scholarly claims


Data can take a broad variety of forms, for example: tables, texts, images, audio/video recordings, archival material and other sources or physical evidence.


Read more at https://eui.libguides.com/research-data-guide/foundational-concepts/what-is-research-data

Research Data Management

Research Data Management (RDM) is an umbrella term covering how you organise, structure, store, and care for the digital information generated or used during a research project

  • Planning how your data will be managed
  • Documenting working practices
  • Handle data on a day-to-day basis
  • Making decisions on what happens to data in the long term
  • Considering whether everything may be reproducible

Read more at https://eui.libguides.com/research-data-guide/foundational-concepts/rdm

The FAIR Principles of research data management

The FAIR guiding principles for scientific data management and stewardship Wilkinson et al., 2016 were developed as guidelines to improve the Findability, Accessibility, Interoperability and Reusability of digital assets; all of which support research reproducibility. The FAIR principles play an important role in making your data available to others (and yourself) for reuse.

The FAIR principles explained

  • Findable: ensuring that your data can be found by both humans and machines, by using a globally unique and persistent identifier (such as a DOI, kind of like an ORCID for data) and standardised, machine-readable metadata.
  • Accessible: once someone has found your data, they need to know how they can get access to them. This could include going through an authorisation and/or authentication process – i.e. it does not have to be open access to be FAIR (ethics always trump openness).
  • Interoperable: the use of open formats ensures that your data can be integrated with other data and that they can be utilised by many applications or workflows for analysis, storage, and processing into the future, regardless of changes in software.
  • Reusable: ensuring that your data (and their related metadata) are openly licensed and well-described, indicating unambiguously how they may be reused without a need to contact the author(s) first.

Research Data Lifecycle

We organise our work around the idea that research data, much like research itself, goes through stages as identified in a research data lifecycle.

Planning for research data

Planning for research data

Planning is a fundamental to ensure that you will consider all conditions and best practices to effectively handle data throughout your research project.

What to consider:

  • How data is collected and generated
  • How data is used, elaborated and organised
  • How data, and data subjects, are protected
  • How data, code and ancillary elements are described and documented
  • How data is stored and secured during the project
  • How dataset authorship and credit are assigned
  • How data outputs is archived and under what terms, can be shared

Read more at https://eui.libguides.com/research-data-guide/plan

Data Management Plan (DMP)

A data management plan (DMP) is a formal document that outlines how research data will be handled throughout a project’s lifecycle.

  • DMPs are increasingly recognised as essential tools for researchers, as they ensure data integrity and facilitate compliance with legal, ethical, and funding requirements.
  • DMPs are described as living documents, meaning they can and should be updated throughout the research project.

Preparing a DMP at the beginning of a research endeavour is an important step in ensuring transparency and reproducibility of research.

Tip

Early-stage researchers (like EUI PhD Researchers) are strongly invited to learn how to prepare a DMP, as they will be required when applying for future project funding.

Discover, access, collect data

Discover, access, collect data

Whether you are collecting original data or reusing existing data, the discovery, access and collection phase lays the foundation for the quality of both the data and its documentation.

While data collection methods depend on the field and research subject, it is important to consider:

  • Options for reusing existing data instead of generating new data
  • Assessment of the quality and reliability of the data sources
  • if working with sensitive or confidential data, data protection and security.
  • If working with human-related data, permissions or informed consent .
  • If reusing existing data, copyright, licenses and terms of use limitations

Copyright and terms of use

Access to, and use of, databases provided by the EUI Library are subject to contractual license agreements and database copyright terms.

  • Data users are individually responsible for compliance with terms and conditions of access and use.
  • Violation of license terms puts at risk other EUI members’ future access to data resources and may expose the university to financial penalties.
  • EUI users may not distribute, or allow any other party to have access to data which is provided under license.

Warning

  • In some cases, the sharing of micro-socioeconomic data with journal referees is not permitted (check individual data contracts for details).
  • Users may not use licensed materials for commercial purposes.

Document, organise, store data

Document your data

Data documentation could be defined as the clear description of everything that a new “data user” or “your future-self” would need to know in order to find, understand, reproduce and reuse your data, independently.

Clear and accurate documentation should include:

  • Purpose, context and methodology of the research project
  • Description of the dataset (structure, folders, files, variables and versioning)
  • Definitions, variable names, problematic values, missing observations etc.
  • Methodology and how and when the data was collected or generated
  • Elaboration techniques (sub-setting, combining &c.)

Tip

  • Good documentation helps make datasets findable, accessible, interoperable and re-usable (FAIR principles).
  • At the end of the research project, codebooks, questionnaires and data dictionaries should be archived with the data. Read more

File naming

Consider the following elements1

  • Version number;
  • Date of creation (date format should be YYYY-MM-DD);
  • Name of creator;
  • Description of content;
  • Name of research team/department associated with the data;
  • Publication date;
  • Project number.

Bast practice:

  • Create meaningful but brief names;
  • Use file names to classify types of files;
  • Avoid using spaces, dots and special characters (& or ? or !);
  • Use hyphens (-) or underscores (_) to separate elements in a file name;
  • Avoid very long file names;
  • Reserve the 3-letter file extension for application-specific codes of file format (e.g. .doc, .xls, .mov, .tif);
  • Include versioning of file names where appropriate.

  1. CESSDA Data Management Expert Guide

Folder structure

The folder structure gives an overview of which information can be found where, enabling present as well as future stakeholders to understand what files have been produced in the project.

Folders should:

  • Follow a structure with folders and subfolders that correspond to the project design and workflow
  • Have a self-explanatory name that is only as long as is necessary
  • Have a unique name – avoid assigning the same name to a folder and a subfolder

Tip

The top folder should have a README.txt file describing the folder structure and what files are contained within the folders.

Read more at https://eui.libguides.com/research-data-guide/document-organise-store/file-naming-folder-structure

Version control: how to keep track of changes in data and code

Version control is the practice of tracking and managing changes to files over time (who does what and when), and is an established practice in software development but can be applied effectively in research.

  • Nothing that is committed to version control is ever lost, unless you work really, really hard at it.
  • We have a record of who made what changes when, and, if needed, revert to a previous version.

Tip

Version control is like an unlimited ‘undo/redo’.

Version control in SSH research

  • Enhanced reproducibility: Version control provides a complete audit trail of the entire research process, from data cleaning to statistical analysis and writing, making it easier to reproduce findings. 

  • Improved collaboration: Teams can work on the same project simultaneously. The system can notify users of conflicts and can help prevent accidental overwrites, ensuring everyone’s work is saved and can be combined efficiently. 

  • Historical record: It creates a detailed history of every change, including who made the change and when, which is invaluable for understanding the project’s evolution. 

  • Efficient error correction: If an error is introduced, it’s easy to identify when it was made and revert to a previous, stable version, much like an “undo” function. 

  • Backup and security: It serves as a secure backup of your work, and platforms like Githib (next slide) can sync projects across multiple devices. 

How to version control

The most common tools to introduce version control in your research workflow are Git and its online, cloud-based, counterparts (e.g. GiHub or GitLab):

  • Git: Free and Open Source (FOSS) tool that can run on most modern operating systems (Microsoft Windows, Apple MacOS, all Linux distributions). Git is integrated in several platforms including Rstudio Desktop, Microsoft VS Code, Matlab, and Overleaf.
  • GitHub: a commercial cloud-based counterpart of Git that supports backup, collaboration, and work across multiple devices. There is a desktop application that functions as a GUI for Git: GitHub Desktop
  • GitLab: similar to GitHub in functionalities, but also released as a Free and Open Source (FOSS) platform

Read more at https://eui.libguides.com/research-data-guide/document-organise-store/version-control

Store your data (safely)

Your data is an important research asset, and its care starts with a proper storage strategy that reduces to minimal any accidental loss.

Never ever leave your data only on your personal computer:

  • Personal hardware breaks or can be stolen
  • Physical incidents happen
  • Accidental deletion happens

Warning

If you are working with personal and sensitive data, you may need extra layers of security!

  • Data integration

  • Transcription
  • Anonymisation
  • Pseudonymisation
  • Format conversion
  • Etc. etc.

Read more at https://eui.libguides.com/research-data-guide/document-organise-store/file-naming-folder-structure

Process: Transcription

If your data are contained in audio (or video) files (e.g. because you collected interviews) in order to analyse your data you may need to create a transcriptions first.

The EUI does not have an officially licensed transcription software, but this Open Source alternative seems well extablished within the academic community and compliant with GDPR:

  • aTrain: aTrain is a tool for automatically transcribing speech recordings utilizing state-of-the-art machine learning models without uploading any data. It was developed by researchers at the Business Analytics and Data Science-Center at the University of Graz and tested by researchers from the Know-Center Graz.

Process: Anonymisation

When data are anonymous, individual research participants or third persons cannot be identified based on indirect identifiers or by combining the data with information available elsewhere.

  • Remove personal data identifiers: Names, postcodes, workplaces, telephone numbers &c.
  • Aggregate, or reduce, the precision of variables: Year of birth rather than birth date, Country rather than city
  • Timing of anonymisation: At the time of data collection / During elaboration (data processing) / At the time of sharing

Process: Pseudonymisation

Pseudonymous data do not allow the identification of a data subject without the use of separately stored additional information.

When data are pseudonymised, unique records are replaced by consistent values either derived from the original values or independent of them so that specific data subjects are no longer identifiable.

  • Justify why data are not anonymised: e.g. follow-up wave of survey
  • Replace personal identifiers: e.g. names with unique identifiers (codes)
  • Store/encrypt pseudonyms separately: keep them secure
  • Pseudonymised data can be anonymised: by destroying decryption key.

Caution

Data should be deleted at end of retention period if not anonymised.

Register, archive, share your data

Why it matters

Making your dataset as open as possible and as closed as necessary, and in line with the FAIR Principles, carries a lot of benefits:

  • Increased impact: Makes data easier to find and reuse, which can lead to new findings, increased citations, and broader recognition of your work.
  • Improved reliability and reproducibility: Allows other researchers to verify your results and helps ensure conclusions are built on a solid foundation. 
  • Enhanced collaboration: Increases opportunities to work with other researchers, institutions, and industries, fostering new partnerships. 
  • Efficiency: Reduces redundant efforts and time-consuming manual data handling by making data more automated and reproducible.  
  • Builds public trust: Increases transparency and allows the public to check the work behind research conclusions

Read more at https://eui.libguides.com/research-data-guide/register-archive-share

Cadmus, the EUI Research Repository

Cadmus, the EUI Research Repository, collects, preserves, and provides access to the EUI research outputs

Cadmus, includes the EUI Research Data collection, where all members of the EUI community can register, archive and share original dataset created during their research projects.

Tip

Three options: 1) Register your data; 2) Register and archive your data; 3) Register, archive and share your data

Register your data

Registering your dataset in a research repository ensures that your data can be found by both humans and machines by assigning a unique and persistent identifier (such as a DOI or HANDLE) and standardised, machine-readable metadata.

Criteria for registering your dataset:

  • You are an active member of the EUI community
  • Your dataset is the result of an original data collection OR its structure carries significant intellectual effort to make it an original work

We recommend registration as a minimum step, but we strongly encourage you to also fully archive your data in Cadmus, as described in Option 2.

Tip

If your dataset is already archived in a third-party trusted repository (e.g. Harvard Dataverse, Zenodo, etc.), you can register it in Cadmus and a link to the archived data will be added.

Register and archive

As you register your dataset in Cadmus (i.e. make a description of the dataset available online), you can also archive your data files, documentation files and related code. Cadmus will take care of their long-term preservation.

Criteria for archiving:

  • Your dataset is registered in Cadmus (prerequisite).
  • If you used data under copyright, please abide to the original terms of use and obtain clearance

Important

While the description of your dataset will always be open, you can explicitly select the access conditions to your data file.

Register, archive and share

Once your dataset are registered and archived in Cadmus, and your dataset qualifies (e.g. does not contain personal and sensitive data according to GDPR), you can make it open and enable its full reuse for the broadest academic community and society.

In Cadmus you can choose:

  • Open —assigning a Creative Commons Attribution 4.0 International (CC-BY) or Public Domain 1.0 Universal (CC0) license
  • Embargoed —you pick a date in the future to open your dataset, licenses above still apply
  • Closed — under this condition, your dataset will stay closed by default, but you can specify under which conditions it can be shared (e.g. )

Tip

If your dataset contains sensitive and/or personal data, consider create an anonymised version
